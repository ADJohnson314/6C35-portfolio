Subtheme: Historical Harms, Contemporary Effects

This analysis covers data on the existence of Redlining in Massachusetts. Redlining was the act of restricting loans to homes and buildings in certain regions of the country, based on the racial demographics of people in each region. This lead to a huge gap in wealth and access to homes between people of different races, which began in the 30's and is still experienced today, even though redlining was later outlawed in the 60's. This dataset shows where in Greater Boston redlining occured, including what regions were labeled as desirable or undesirable to issue loans to, as well as what type of buildings (commercial, industrial, or residential) were in each region.

Overall Analysis Questions

  1. What races or ethnic groups were affected the most by redlining?

    A good starting point in the exploration of this data is to compare the magnitudes of penalties from redlining between different races, to get a better idea of which people were considered most undeserving of loans, and who would benefit the most from any measures taken to undo the effects of redlining. Since the readings seem to focus more on how Black citizens were disadvantaged by redlining, my initial guess is that harsher ratings will be more prevalent in predominantly Black regions of Massachusetts.

  2. What correlation, if any, is there between the type of buildings in a region and the rating of that region?

    I'd like to seek an answer to this question because it would show whether the effects of redlining extended beyond restricting access to housing. If commercial and industrial buildings were underfunded because of the presence of racial minorities, this might indicate that those citizens were given less opportunities for jobs than white citizens.

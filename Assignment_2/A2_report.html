Subtheme: Zoning and Housing Supply

One of the barriers preventing people from having affordable housing is the set of regulations on what types of buildings can be constructed in a certain area, called zoning. In the past, these regulations have been used to restrict certain types of people from owning homes in different parts of Massachusetts, usually by only allowing single-family homes to be built in an area, which are more expensive than other types of housing. This analysis covers data on where zoning exists in the state, including the percentage of single-family homes in each municipality, and the race, age, and other demographics of the people living in an area. All municipalities in the dataset are under zoning.

Overall Analysis Questions

  1. How have different groups of people been affected by exclusive zoning on housing, in the form of restricting building types to single-family homes?

    I would like to compare the population of people living in municipalities where only single-family houses are allowed to the population of people living on less restrictive municipalities. This will better reveal the effects and likely motives behind zoning practices in Massachusetts.

  2. How does the total number of households in a municipality change as the percentage of single-family zoning change?

    One of the consequences of zoning is that there are fewer homes in Massachusetts that are available to own and live in. I am curious of just how severe the regulation of single-family homes is towards reducing the number of existing homes, specifically how quickly does the number of homes drop as the amount of zoning increases.

  3. What are the gaps in income between people who live in single-family zoning municipalities versus people who don't?

    This question aims to show just how important the presence of municipalities without zoning is in allowing people to afford homes. This analysis should consider how certain demographics affect this gap as well.
